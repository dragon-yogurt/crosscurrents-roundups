CROSSCURRENTS
Briefing · Week of May 30 – June 6, 2026

A 60-second scan of the week's news, for readers who haven't been following.

The Maine Senate primary scandal

  • Graham Platner, the Marine veteran leading Maine's Democratic Senate primary, was hit with three damaging stories: sexting, old Reddit posts, and a Nazi-coded tattoo.
  • The sexting leak came from Platner's own campaign opposition file — meaning a Democratic insider engineered it.
  • Maine law lets state party officials pick a replacement nominee if a primary winner withdraws by July 13. The timing matches.
  • Polls show Platner ahead of five-term Republican Sen. Susan Collins, but Maine surveys have overstated Democrats by about 4 points since 2014.

Texas and the Senate map

  • In Texas, Democrat James Talarico — a state legislator and Presbyterian seminarian — now faces Republican Ken Paxton, the state attorney general and an election denier with a donor-corruption case.
  • Paxton's nomination opened the first credible Democratic path to a Texas Senate seat. No Democrat has won there since 1988.

The anti-monopoly movement splits

  • Economist Noah Smith publicly broke with the Lina Khan / Barry Lynn anti-monopoly movement, calling it anti-empirical and factional after the Biden-era FTC went after low-margin grocers and airlines.
  • Matt Stoller framed cable mogul John Malone (Liberty Media) as the missing link between mid-century capitalism and today's tech oligarchy.
  • Stoller separately argued SpaceX's $1.8 trillion IPO is rigged: only 5% of shares will float, retail gets 30% of the float, NASDAQ-100 entry in 15 days.
  • Trump's antitrust chief argued state attorneys general are now the front line, recovering an 1870s tradition of state-level antitrust.
  • A new 10-12.5% tariff hit 60 countries (including China and India) for failing to bar forced-labor imports — grounded in U.S. tariff statutes dating to 1890.
  • Yglesias documented that since 2012, U.S. assets have soared while wages flatlined — a $1 million S&P inheritance from 2012 is now worth about $6 million.

AI policy after the Pope's encyclical

  • Reaction to Pope Leo XIV's first encyclical Magnifica Humanitas (covered last week) produced a three-way split: build it as public infrastructure, name specific uses as sins, or prohibit it outright.
  • 71% of Americans now oppose a data center being built near their home — a 49-point swing in nine months. Rural opposition exceeds urban.
  • More than 20 data center projects were canceled in early 2026, wiping out $41 billion in planned investment.
  • A blinded study of 16 law professors found large language models beat human-written legal tutoring answers 75% of the time.
  • Author Freddie deBoer named a new AI failure mode he calls "social deference" — the model gets the right answer, then mislabels it to match the user's mistake.
  • Historian Adam Tooze invoked a horse analogy: UK working-horse population collapsed 98% after the steam engine. Similar displacement could hit humans within a decade.

Iran's aftermath

  • A pseudonymous Iranian-American writer argued the Iranian regime is more entrenched than before the spring U.S.-Israeli airstrikes that killed senior officials.
  • Ukraine's drone campaign has matured into a 2,000-kilometer strategic effort permanently shrinking Russian oil output by roughly 600,000 to 800,000 barrels per day.
  • Oil-and-gas executives are now hiring SEAL-veteran bodyguards as gas-price backlash builds. One utility's disclosed security spending jumped 4,767%.
  • The U.S. Treasury froze $344 million of an Iran-linked stablecoin (a dollar-pegged digital token) — proving the digital-dollar workaround to sanctions is itself freezable on demand.
  • Germany lost its routine bid for a UN Security Council seat for the first time. European press read it as a Global South rebuke over Berlin's quiet support for Israel.
  • In a long interview, analyst Ian Bremmer substituted Thucydides's "rising-power" framework for the "Gracchi trap" — late-Republican Rome's pattern of internal grievance revolts wearing out the system from inside.

Institutions under audit

  • The Supreme Court used a fast unsigned emergency order to let Alabama proceed with a congressional map two Trump-appointed lower-court judges had ruled intentionally racially discriminatory.
  • The Justice Department extracted a felony guilty plea from former Trump national-security adviser John Bolton over classified notes used in his anti-Trump memoir.
  • Political scientist Francis Fukuyama traced judicial independence to the 11th-century church-state struggle over who appointed bishops, calling Trump's posture a rejection of Western economic modernity's substrate.
  • A 67-bridge dataset showed U.S. construction times have nearly doubled since the 1940s — from 3.1 years to 5.4 — with planning times unchanged.
  • California counted 70% of ballots three days after the 2024 election; the 50-state average was 95%. India counted 640 million votes in one day.
  • British military procurement is broken. An armored-vehicle program is ten years behind schedule. Submarine bolts have been reassembled with glue.

The family and wage debate

  • Columnist Ezra Klein interviewed Atlantic writer Helen Lewis on "masculinism" — an institutional ideological project (distinct from the Andrew Tate online persona) reaching into Trump staff and the Pentagon.
  • A Compact essay traced a new genre — "heteropessimist horror" films like Obsession — in which marriage itself is depicted as the monster.
  • Economist Tyler Cowen flagged a study finding European countries adopting "tacit consent" sexual-assault laws saw a 4% fertility decline via risk-averse withdrawal from dating.
  • Former Vice President Mike Pence's Free Press manifesto attacking Trump's second term as a break from Reagan-Goldwater conservatism reads widely as a 2028 presidential launch.
  • Economist Matt Bruenig recalculated U.S. labor taxes by counting employer health insurance as a tax, and found middle-class Americans already pay Nordic effective rates.
  • Reshma Saujani (Girls Who Code founder) argued universal childcare is the bipartisan-popular policy both parties refuse to pass.

Post-liberal governance abroad

  • An Italian writer's CV-pattern analysis of three ministers in Hungary's new Magyar government found Shell, U.S. LNG firms, and Atlantic-aligned think tanks dominating the backgrounds.
  • French commentator Aurelien argued forty years of neoliberalism stripped the community substrate that let Britain endure 1939-45, making future rationing or conscription politically impossible.
  • Wall Street Journal columnist Joseph Sternberg read Beijing's loosening of internal-migration rules (the hukou system) as defensive politics, not the consumption boost Western analysts expect.
  • An African analyst walked the January collapse of a Kenyan biofuel startup whose investor of record was a major tax haven, as a case study in colonial-template carbon economies.
  • China Digital Times documented a Shanxi mine explosion: 247 workers underground but only 124 on the official roster. The death toll moved from 4 to 82 within hours.
  • China Digital Times also catalogued roughly 40 search terms blocked on the 37th anniversary of the Tiananmen Square crackdown, including a newly added code for "polarization."

Smaller items worth noting

  • A Free Press exclusive: the Justice Department is preparing to indict a Guatemalan woman for fraudulently taking custody of at least eight migrant children between 2019 and 2023.
  • Pastor Ezra Jin, leader of one of China's largest underground churches, was denied legal access despite Trump's recent claim that Xi was considering his release.
  • Yglesias argued Poland's success — now the world's 20th-largest economy, with universal healthcare and high minimum wage — disproves the U.S. right's claim that European social policy kills growth.
  • Noah Smith argued the U.S. treatment of 73 million pigs in industrial farms is a moral failure, and condemned a federal bill that would override state animal-welfare laws.

The Bottom Line

  • Two patterns ran through the week.
  • First, the apparatus that used to do persuasion and recruitment — for parties, antitrust, foreign policy — is now substituting press leaks, emergency orders, and procedural workarounds for the capacities it has lost.
  • Second, the substrate beneath politics — wages, marriage rates, refining capacity, ballot counting, defense industry — is failing in ways the surface debates rarely reach.
  • The most durable finding is the cross-orientation convergence: writers from incompatible coordinates keep arriving at the same structural diagnosis, even when they disagree about what to do.
