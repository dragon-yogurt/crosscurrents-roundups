CROSSCURRENTS
Briefing · Week of June 22–27, 2026

A 60-second scan of the week's news, for readers who haven't been following.

A Left Insurgency Sweeps New York, and the Democrats Split Over What It Means

  • The clearest event of the week was an election result. In New York City's Democratic primary on June 23, all three congressional candidates endorsed by Zohran Mamdani — the city's left-wing mayor and a member of the Democratic Socialists of America — won. Two unseated establishment-backed incumbents, one of them the chair of the Congressional Hispanic Caucus.
    • The party's own leaders backed the losers and delivered none of them: Hakeem Jeffries, the top House Democrat; Chuck Schumer, the Senate Democratic leader; and Kathy Hochul, New York's governor.
  • What happened isn't in dispute; what it means is the whole argument. Commentators across the spectrum agreed on two facts: the left insurgency is real, not a fluke, and the establishment can no longer guarantee which of its own candidates win primaries.
  • The split is one question — is a surging socialist left an asset or a liability?
    • One camp sees a mirror of the MAGA takeover of the Republicans: a hard-left faction that wins primaries on anti-Trump anger but repels the swing voters needed to win general elections.
    • The opposite camp sees the only force willing to confront concentrated corporate and financial power, and asks only whether the winners can govern.
    • Both camps share one worry: the wins may not turn into governing competence.
  • A sharper line runs inside the left. The durable version is practical municipal delivery — cheaper transit, frozen rents. The self-defeating version is a purist, academic strain: one new winner, a prison abolitionist, wouldn't say whether murderers should be jailed — the kind of candidate that invites the backlash that sank the last left surge after 2018.
  • Underneath sits a structural diagnosis of why the party keeps producing leaders who can't win tough races. As competitive statewide races vanished, persuading undecided voters became a lost skill and keeping the party's factions happy became the whole game.
    • The backdrop to Gavin Newsom, California's governor and a 2028 contender, who spent the week loudly praising the deeply unpopular former president Joe Biden — a costly loyalty signal to the party base, read by at least one analyst as weakness, not strength.

The Iran War's Aftermath: Who Came Out Ahead, and a Rising Anti-War Faction

  • Last week the four-month U.S.-Iran war ended in a ceasefire; this week the argument moved from who won to who lost. Nearly everyone reached for the same word — "humiliation" — and pinned it on a different culprit.
  • Beneath the disagreement, a striking convergence. Writers who want America out of Middle East wars and writers who despise the Iranian regime reached the same verdict: the Islamic Republic survived intact, and Washington is now the side asking for terms.
    • Iran traded a reopened Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway carrying ~20% of the world's oil — and a reported $12 billion in unfrozen assets for nothing lasting. Its nuclear program endures; regime change is off the table.
    • Several critics noted the irony: this is materially worse than the 2015 nuclear deal Trump tore up in 2018 and spent years denouncing.
  • Hawks and the anti-war camp split on what the same outcome proves.
    • Hawks say American restraint threw away a winnable war — one claimed staged Gulf troops could have seized islands and choked off Iran's oil at low risk. But that the regime would have folded is asserted, not shown; a writer on the other side named exactly that move — deciding the enemy's strategy for him — as the hawks' central fallacy.
    • A few hawks cited an Iranian drone strike on a cargo ship in the Strait on June 26 as proof the regime can't be trusted — but a single incident can't settle a claim that large.
  • The forward-looking story is the rise of the "restrainers" — the faction on the right that wants the U.S. out of foreign wars. Its standard-bearer is Vice President JD Vance.
    • Vance now owns the post-war diplomacy, including a new channel for Iranian and American military officers to manage disputes from Doha, Qatar's capital.
    • He spent the week subtly distancing himself from Trump on tone, immigration, and Israel — read as an early, unofficial 2028 campaign, built on the fact that an elected VP can't be fired. Even some left-leaning writers said his "America First moderation" should be taken seriously.
  • The war's costs landed hardest off-camera. The one piece tracing the shock onto a bystander economy looked at Somalia, in the Horn of Africa: fuel prices up ~77%, shipping freight roughly doubled as vessels rerouted the long way around Africa.

AI Data Centers Become Kitchen-Table Politics

  • For two years, AI — software that learns from data to generate text, images, and answers — was discussed as a marvel. This week the backlash over its physical footprint became top-tier politics. The grievance is concrete: who pays for the enormous electricity that AI data centers (warehouse-sized buildings full of computers) now demand.
  • The American fight is over electricity bills, and the call for a pause now comes from inside senior policymaking.
    • Frank Pallone, the top Democrat on the House committee that oversees energy, demanded a national moratorium on new data centers until harms to air, water, and power bills are prevented.
    • Public opinion moved fast: the share of Americans blaming data centers for rising power prices jumped from 28% to 53% in under a year.
    • The national evidence is mixed, but the harm is real in New Jersey, where an independent grid monitor attributed $23 billion in added costs to data-center demand.
    • In Europe, during a record June heatwave, national newspapers named AI directly as a climate villain for its thirst for power and water.
  • A separate strand asked not who pays but who owns. Senator Bernie Sanders proposed taxing the major AI companies half their stock into public hands.
    • Understood plainly: not the government buying companies, but a one-time tax paid in shares (~$1 trillion in value), turning firms like Anthropic and OpenAI into part-public companies — on the model of Norway's state-owned oil giant.
  • The week's clearest signal: across very different politics, almost no serious voice now argues the market should simply be left alone. The fight is over which lever to pull.

Two Immigration Rulings and a Fight Over Who Belongs

  • America's 250th birthday arrived with two Supreme Court immigration decisions and a new federal rule — treated as one question: who belongs to the American political community, and on what basis.
  • Both rulings, decided 6 to 3, turned on reading the words Congress actually wrote.
    • The Court upheld "metering" — turning migrants back on the Mexican side of the border before they can request asylum — reasoning that a migrant stopped before setting foot on U.S. soil hasn't "arrived in" the country, so the right to a hearing never triggers.
    • The Court declined to second-guess the administration's decision to end Temporary Protected Status (a humanitarian designation letting nationals of disaster- or conflict-hit countries stay temporarily), because the governing law explicitly bars courts from reviewing it.
    • Defenders called both straightforward applications of statute, not political favors.
  • An even-handed test is built into that defense. The same conservative legal writer who praised these wins predicts the administration will lose the pending birthright-citizenship case — because the Fourteenth Amendment's plain words guarantee citizenship to nearly everyone born on U.S. soil. Reading the text is a method, not a team.
  • The membership question also surfaced as paperwork. A forthcoming Health and Human Services rule would tighten vetting of adults claiming unaccompanied migrant children — narrowing acceptable ID, ending background-check waivers, adding checks for gang tattoos — after a run of fraud cases. The government concedes the tradeoff: genuine relatives will find it harder too.

A Worry That Spans the Spectrum: The West Can't Steer Its Own Governments

  • In Britain, Prime Minister Keir Starmer resigned, and the week marked ten years since the 2016 Brexit vote to leave the European Union. Writers from incompatible starting points converged on one diagnosis: the machinery of government no longer answers to whoever nominally controls it.
  • A Leave voter and a Remain voter, in the same magazine, agreed Brexit's "take back control" promise failed — and disagreed only on why.
    • One located the unsteerable force outside Britain, in the EU's economic gravity: it buys ~40% of British exports, so Britain must follow EU rules either way — now with no vote in writing them.
    • The other located it inside Britain, in a permanent administrative state that outlasts every elected government.
    • A third named the mechanism: a "para-state" of courts, regulators, and arm's-length agencies largely beyond elected control.
  • Britain churned through six prime ministers in a decade. One writer called that not unique dysfunction but unusual honesty about a West-wide condition: France's government is paralyzed, and the hard-right Alternative for Germany leads German polls.
  • The one apparent counter-model just collapsed. Hungary under Viktor Orbán was the state that openly fought to seize control of its own institutions — a move admirers on the American right hoped to copy. But Orbán lost his recent election, and the incoming government is reversing him. He fell not on too little state power but on a stalled economy and corruption.
  • This is the domestic face of the limit the Iran restrainers describe abroad: state power has hard limits, and can't simply remake other societies — or, it turns out, reliably steer its own.

The Bottom Line

  • The week's stories share one shape: a loss of control over the machine.
    • A political party can no longer pick its own candidates.
    • A government can no longer steer its own bureaucracy.
    • Across the spectrum, almost no one believes a fast-moving AI industry can be safely left to the market alone.
    • Even the war fits: a superpower that couldn't compel a much weaker state to fold.
  • The most useful insight comes from the Western-governance writers: the failure isn't a shortage of authority but a shortage of durability. Governments can still act; what they can't do is make a decision stick, sustain it, or correct it. Every proposed remedy needs the very capacity they say is gone.
  • A quieter thread runs beneath: while the West argues over who controls its institutions, China is out-building it on the physical technologies of the next era — the batteries, motors, and power electronics behind electric cars, drones, and robots. The U.S. keeps its AI-software lead, but a data center full of geniuses can't build a drone without the hardware.
    • The catch, from a Chinese dissident writer: the same electrified economy absorbs a brutal human cost — lead-poisoned children, cars that burn with occupants inside — that citizens are pressured to bear in silence. Industrial capacity measures what a country can make, not whether its system is worth copying.
  • When analysts who start from incompatible premises keep landing on the same diagnosis — that the establishment, whether a party, a state, or a market, has lost the ability to steer — the agreement is the signal, and the shouting is the noise.
