A briefing for someone who hasn’t been following.

The Iran War Settles Into a Stalemate Nobody Knows How to End

  • The U.S.-Iran war began February 28 with American strikes on Iranian nuclear and military sites; this week marked Day 70. There is technically a cease-fire signed April 8. It is not holding.
    • On May 4, Iran fired drones and missiles at the United Arab Emirates and U.S. ships in the Persian Gulf.
    • The U.S. Navy sank six Iranian fast-attack boats in response.
    • On May 5 the administration “paused” Project Freedom, the U.S. Central Command operation to escort some 2,000 commercial ships stranded by dueling blockades through the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world’s oil moves.
    • By Friday May 8, the two sides were exchanging fire again. Trump described one strike as “a love tap” and continued to insist the cease-fire was intact.
  • The economic shape of the stalemate is now legible.
    • Iranian inflation: 62% overall, 99% on food.
    • Iranian rial collapsing toward 2 million to the dollar; foreign reserves of about $26 billion can fund six to fourteen months of blockade conditions.
    • But Iran has alternatives — overland trade with neighbors, barter for oil, several years of accumulated cryptocurrency capacity, and Chinese commodity purchases absorbing roughly 30% of its exports.
    • The American side is no more comfortable: Brent crude near $100/barrel (it traded near $70 before the war); 21 U.S. warships in the region.
    • One analyst’s economic model: if the strait stays closed through September, the U.S. faces inflation near 5% and a likely recession.
  • Writers paying close attention disagree on which side runs out of room first.
    • Time favors the U.S.: Iran’s economy is breaking, U.S. technology will prevail.
    • Time favors Iran: U.S. munitions stockpiles are also depleting, the Iranian regime has consolidated rather than fractured under bombing, and the strait cannot be reopened militarily because Iran can sink any tanker that tries to cross.
    • A third reading from a Wall Street Journal report this week: Saudi Arabia and Kuwait blocked U.S. military use of their bases for the escort operation. Saudi-Iranian competition may be the war’s most consequential dynamic, with the U.S. providing the hardware. Riyadh pushed Trump to launch the war; Riyadh just blocked his exit.
  • A separate strand from The Atlantic on the Iranian exile-monarchist movement — sometimes positioned by Western commentators as a plausible regime successor — finds:
    • Movement led by Reza Pahlavi, 65-year-old son of the deposed Shah.
    • Canadian police have charged two of his followers with the first-degree murder of a Vancouver-based mathematician who was a public critic.
    • Coordinated online-harassment tactics and demands for total loyalty have fractured the base.
    • Pahlavi remains genuinely popular inside Iran but as “an absence of something rather than an existence” — a Second-Coming-shaped void rather than a credible governing alternative.

Britain Has a New Dominant Party, and Labour May Not Survive

  • The May 7-8 local elections in England, Wales, and Scotland were the most consequential British political result in two decades.
    • Keir Starmer’s Labour government — elected with a historic 411-seat majority less than two years ago — lost more than 1,000 council seats.
    • Labour also lost the Welsh parliament for the first time since its founding in 1999.
    • Reform UK, the right-populist party led by Nigel Farage, won roughly one in three contested seats. In Hartlepool, Reform took every council seat.
    • The Greens hit Labour from the left over Israel and Lebanon; the Liberal Democrats picked up centrist defectors. Labour was bleeding from every direction.
  • The structural cause is the immigration backlash that has dominated British politics since the 2021-22 surge in arrivals during the late Boris Johnson period.
    • British voters now rate immigration as their most important issue more than any other electorate worldwide (Gallup).
    • Compounding troubles: a £26 billion tax-rise budget that broke campaign promises, an Epstein-tainted ambassador appointment to Washington, an anemic economy.
    • Starmer’s personal approval dropped below his Conservative predecessor’s within three months of taking office.
  • What’s notable is the speed. France’s Socialist Party took five years to collapse from governing to marginal after François Hollande’s 2012 election. Britain may compress that into a single Parliament.
    • Labour’s 2024 landslide was structurally fragile — median majority per constituency was the lowest since the 1950s; a swing of 100,000-200,000 voters in the right places would have killed it.
    • Starmer’s allies are now briefing journalists that ousting him would trigger a bond-market collapse. That his strongest argument for keeping his job is that markets fear his successor more is itself the diagnosis.

The Court Decision That Already Reshaped American Maps

  • Last week the Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in Louisiana v. Callais that race-based congressional districts violate the Constitution.
    • Written by Justice Samuel Alito; applies more broadly than its facts. Most majority-Black districts created since the 1965 Voting Rights Act may be vulnerable.
    • Justice Elena Kagan’s dissent: combined with a 2019 ruling that put partisan gerrymanders beyond federal review, the new decision lets a state legislature dilute Black voting power as long as it claims to be doing so for partisan rather than racial reasons.
  • The Republican legislative response was unusually fast — within a single week.
    • Tennessee enacted legislation splitting the city of Memphis among three congressional districts, dismantling the 40-year-old majority-Black 9th District.
    • Louisiana Governor Jeff Landry invoked emergency powers (typically reserved for natural disasters) to suspend an in-progress House primary mid-vote, after more than 42,000 ballots had already been cast, so the legislature could redraw the map.
    • Alabama Republicans held redistricting votes during a tornado watch with the state capitol flooding.
    • South Carolina is moving to gerrymander the seat of Representative James Clyburn, one of the most prominent Black political figures in the country.
    • President Trump explicitly pressuring states: “If they have to vote twice, so be it.”
  • The picture is not one-sided.
    • Virginia Democrats had passed a constitutional amendment to bypass the state’s independent redistricting commission and impose what political analysts on both sides called one of the most extreme partisan gerrymanders in the country.
    • Virginia’s Supreme Court struck it down this week, finding Democrats had used a procedurally invalid sequence — convening a special session to ratify the amendment after early voting in the relevant assembly elections had already begun.
    • The score is not asymmetric in intent. It is asymmetric in capacity: Republicans control more state legislatures with the procedural majorities to act fast, and the new constitutional architecture rewards exactly that capacity.
  • A statistical aside cuts against a common framing. The supposed trade-off — that breaking up majority-Black districts reduces Black representation — turns out to be largely empirical mythology.
    • Once a district reliably elects a Democrat, the chance of electing a Black member is meaningful even at low Black population share.
    • Reason: nearly half of Democratic House members are non-white versus under 10% of Republicans.
    • Spreading minority voters across multiple safely-blue districts can yield more Black members AND more Democratic seats simultaneously.

A Discount Airline Fails — and Two Writers Cannot Agree on Why

  • Spirit Airlines, the ultra-low-cost carrier, filed to liquidate this week.
    • Spirit lost money, ran out of capital, and is being broken up so its planes, gates, and airport slots can be redistributed.
    • What’s contested is whether this is a market working or a market failing.
  • Reading 1 — market concentration killed it:
    • The U.S. airline industry has consolidated to where three legacy carriers (Delta, United, American) control most busy routes.
    • They use a “basic economy” fare to compete on price while extracting higher revenue from premium passengers on the same plane.
    • Spirit was crowded out by structural advantages the Big Three accumulated through previous mergers, including airport slot allocations that block new entrants.
    • This reading favors antitrust intervention to undo recent airline mergers.
  • Reading 2 — competition working as designed:
    • Airline assets are tangible and easily redeployable. When Spirit’s planes go to other carriers and its gates revert to airport authorities, no value is destroyed.
    • The American market has fewer leisure-only city pairs (small-city-to-resort routes like Liverpool to Mallorca) than Europe, where the original ultra-low-cost model thrives.
    • The Big Three’s basic-economy product is genuinely a workable served-the-customer model.
    • Spirit’s failure looks like a competitive industry doing what competitive industries do.
  • Both writers have data; both have priors. What would settle the disagreement:
    • Post-mortem flight data on whether Spirit’s failed routes were monopolized hubs or genuinely competitive routes.
    • Whether the other surviving low-cost carriers (Frontier, Allegiant) make it through the same Iran-war fuel shock that pushed Spirit over the edge.
  • Political stakes are not small. Airfares are an electoral issue, and the framing question of whether market concentration is the cause sits underneath proposed regulatory and antitrust responses both parties are now considering.

The Most Important Drug Story Since Statins

  • GLP-1 agonists — sold under brand names including Ozempic, Wegovy, and Zepbound — are producing clinical results that go far beyond weight loss.

    • One in eight U.S. adults is now taking one.
    • Originally developed for type 2 diabetes.

  • The headline finding from recent trials:

    • In patients without diabetes, GLP-1s reduce cardiovascular events (heart attacks and strokes) by roughly 20% independent of any weight they help patients lose.
    • That benefit is comparable to statins (the cholesterol drugs that became standard preventive medicine in the 1990s, which reduce cardiovascular events by about 29%).

  • The mechanism appears to be more than appetite suppression.

    • The drugs reach the brain via the bloodstream and act on the region that signals “there’s a toxin in circulation” (which is why nausea is the most common side effect).
    • But also direct effects on inflammation and on liver, kidney, and possibly addiction-related signaling.
    • One leading researcher’s three-bucket model: weight loss, inflammation modulation, direct organ-signaling — each contributing independently.
    • Early signs the drugs may reduce alcohol consumption, gambling impulses, and other compulsive behaviors.

  • This matters beyond medical specialists. If trials hold up, the framing of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and several related conditions as primarily a function of willpower and lifestyle becomes obsolete — in roughly the way the willpower framing became obsolete for high cholesterol once statins existed.

  • Open political and economic questions the policy system has not begun to address:

    • Telemedicine and Chinese-manufactured peptide gray markets are already widespread.
    • Comprehensive insurance coverage has not arrived.
    • The food-environment debate (whether public policy should aggressively reshape what Americans eat through soda taxes, school lunch reform, and similar measures) remains unresolved.

The AI Boom Hits Two Real Walls

  • Local political resistance to data centers is now substantial.
    • At least 20 data center projects worth a combined $41.7 billion and 3.5 gigawatts of electricity demand were canceled after local opposition in the first three months of 2026.
    • That is a record for any single quarter and is on pace to exceed all of 2025 within five months.
    • About 100 new local data-center fights have been added to a national tracking database in the same period.
    • Some cancellations involved facilities that did not even run AI models.
    • Some involved developers who preemptively addressed water use, noise, and setback concerns and lost the planning vote anyway.
    • Pattern is bipartisan: a Republican county in Vice President Vance’s home state and a Democratic-leaning Florida county both rejected major projects within months of each other.
  • The Trump administration is halting onshore wind via a permitting workaround.
    • The Department of Defense’s routine sign-off for onshore wind turbine projects has stopped.
    • Result: as many as 165 wind farms on indefinite hold.
    • The Pentagon’s radar-interference review is normally a formality completed in days; developers report it has become an open-ended block, including for projects far from any military base.
    • Separately: the administration paid French oil major TotalEnergies roughly $1 billion to abandon two U.S. offshore wind leases — a deal now facing congressional investigation, California regulatory inquiry, and a New York state pension fund divestment review.
    • The siege strategy is not to win on merits but to make the regulatory environment impossible to invest in. Developers expected to litigate.
  • Together these stories complicate the dominant AI narrative.
    • The infinite-demand assumption — that compute capacity can scale to whatever the market wants because the economic returns will justify any electricity bill — does not survive contact with county planning boards in Florida or with a federal permitting apparatus that can be turned off.
    • Five years from now, the most politically consequential effect of AI may not be job displacement but electricity prices, grid strain, and the local fights both produce.

The Government Names New Domestic Enemies

  • Investigative journalist Ken Klippenstein this week obtained and published the Trump administration’s new National Counterterrorism Strategy — the first such document since the Biden administration’s 2021 version.
    • Three current terror-threat categories: “Narcoterrorists and Transnational Gangs,” “Legacy Islamist Terrorists,” and — new in this iteration — “Violent Left-Wing Extremists, including Anarchists and Anti-Fascists.”
    • The document commits the federal government to “rapid identification and neutralization of violent secular political groups whose ideology is anti-American, radically pro-transgender, and anarchist.”
    • “All the tools constitutionally available” will be used to “map them at home” and “cripple them operationally before they can maim or kill the innocent.”
    • Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has pledged to deploy financial-mapping techniques originally developed against al-Qaeda against domestic political networks.
  • The document invokes the September 2025 assassination of conservative activist Charlie Kirk at Utah Valley University as part of its rationale, citing “the assassination of Charlie Kirk by a radical who espoused extreme transgender ideologies.”
    • It includes a sentence stating “our counterterrorism powers will not be used to target our fellow Americans who simply disagree with us.”
    • The operational text immediately following that disclaimer empowers exactly that kind of targeting based on speech and ideology rather than completed acts.
  • This is a substantial shift in federal counterterrorism posture.
    • The post-9/11 federal counterterrorism apparatus was originally built to address foreign Islamist threats and was constrained — sometimes effectively, sometimes notionally — from operating against domestic political groups.
    • The new strategy formalizes its redirection toward Americans whose ideological positions the administration has classified as terror-equivalent.
  • A pairing this week — federal civil-rights enforcement actions against “reverse” discrimination:
    • Department of Justice finding that the UCLA medical school discriminated against white and Asian applicants.
    • Equal Employment Opportunity Commission lawsuit against the New York Times alleging a white male editor was passed over for a promotion.
    • Whether these actions are legitimate enforcement of color-blind anti-discrimination law or politicized application of civil-rights powers depends largely on one’s existing politics.
    • But the pattern across both — counterterrorism document plus enforcement actions — is the federal apparatus being repurposed to act against categories of Americans the previous administration had treated as either off-limits or as victims rather than targets.

The Long Pivot: China, Apple, and Ray Dalio’s “Heart Attack”

  • President Trump’s coming summit with Chinese President Xi Jinping in Beijing is being read two ways.
    • Some commentators: the October 2025 Busan trade cease-fire is Trump going soft on Beijing.
    • Others: beneath the rhetoric, the U.S. is constructing a serious decoupling architecture:
      • The Federal Communications Commission has barred Chinese-made drones and routers from the U.S. market.
      • Restricted Chinese-government-owned labs from testing American electronics (those labs handled an estimated 75% of U.S. electronics certification at the peak of globalization).
      • Proposed barring Chinese state-owned carriers from operating U.S. data centers and points of presence.
  • Apple announced this week that Tim Cook will become executive chairman, with John Ternus succeeding him as CEO this fall.
    • A long investigation by a journalist with extensive China expertise documents that Cook did not merely depend on China’s manufacturing base — he built it.
    • Apple invested an estimated $275 billion upgrading its Chinese suppliers between 2016 and 2021 — more than five times the entire authorization of the 2022 CHIPS Act, which the U.S. government passed to revive American semiconductor manufacturing.
    • Apple Intelligence, the company’s AI software, runs on American iPhones but not on Chinese ones, because Chinese regulatory approval has not arrived.
    • Apple is partnering with Alibaba to build a censored version that will refuse to discuss Tiananmen, Taiwan, Xinjiang, or Communist Party authority.
    • Ternus inherits a company structurally entangled with the PRC in ways no other major American firm matches.
  • Ray Dalio, founder of one of the world’s largest hedge funds, gave a long interview to New York Times opinion columnist Ross Douthat framing what this all means.
    • Dalio has spent decades developing a “big cycle” theory of reserve-currency empire decline across the past 500 years.
    • His argument: the U.S. is following the British post-1945 trajectory faster than its political class has registered.
    • U.S. spending: roughly $7 trillion against $5 trillion in revenue.
    • National debt at roughly six times annual revenue.
    • The post-1945 multilateral order has eroded into a power-based system.
    • The Iran war’s outcome is, on Dalio’s read, the “Suez moment” — the discrete event that crystallizes a pre-existing decline, the way Britain’s failed 1956 attempt to retake the Suez Canal made its loss of empire suddenly legible.
    • Prescription: fiscal compromise the current political system cannot deliver, plus diversifying personal portfolios into gold (now the world’s second-largest central-bank reserve asset).
  • Dalio is making forecasts; forecasts often prove wrong. But the same data — a U.S. fiscal position that is structurally unsustainable, a multilateral order that no longer functions, and a transition in which China is positioning to be the world’s transactional hub even as its own legitimacy is eroding from within — is being read by writers from very different political starting points to roughly the same conclusion.

Russia Breaks Its Own Bargain

  • Russia’s spring wave of mobile-internet shutdowns is now breaking ordinary economic life.
    • Officially justified as protection against Ukrainian drone attacks, but increasingly applied broadly across cities including Moscow.
    • Now breaking ATMs, banking apps, taxi services, even officially approved websites.
  • Public-opinion signals are visible in ways they have not been before:
    • President Putin’s approval dropped below 80% in April for the first time since fall 2022, losing 8 points in seven months.
    • Apolitical celebrity influencers have begun publicly criticizing the shutdowns.
    • Putin himself reportedly told officials to “explain restrictions better” and lawmakers “not to get stuck on prohibitions.”
  • The diagnostic claim is structural.
    • Since the post-2014 Crimea period, the Russian state’s implicit bargain has been: stay apolitical and consumerist, and the state will preserve everyday convenience.
    • Russians have learned to live with political censorship — VPNs are everyday infrastructure, banned Instagram still drives nearly half of Russian influencer income.
    • They have not learned to live with the state breaking the part of the bargain that delivered the consumerist payoff.
    • The new shutdowns invade the private zones where Russians have arranged to hide from politics.
  • The stronger claim — that this represents a threshold of public dissatisfaction the Putin regime has not previously crossed — is less certain.
    • Putin’s approval has fluctuated before; predictions of imminent Russian regime delegitimation have a poor historical track record.
    • But the specific mechanism — that the consumerist bargain itself, not political dissent, is the load-bearing element of regime stability — is a useful frame for reading Russian developments over the next several months.

What to Watch

  • Three threads from this week extend forward:
    • Whether the Iran war’s dynamic shifts when one side hits an economic or political constraint the other can exploit. Most likely vector: U.S. inflation forcing a domestic political reckoning. Alternative: Iranian financial collapse.
    • Whether the post-Callais Republican redistricting wave runs into either federal court intervention or Democratic counter-strategies. The 2026 midterm map could be substantially different from current expectations within months.
    • Whether the Trump-Xi summit produces a bargain or a break, and whether Apple’s leadership transition reveals strategic adjustments the company has not yet announced.
  • Beneath all of this sits the Dalio question: is the United States in a slow structural decline in which discrete events keep crystallizing what was already true, or are the discrete events themselves the cause of cumulative damage? Five years from now, the answer will be obvious. This week, it isn’t.
