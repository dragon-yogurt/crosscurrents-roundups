CROSSCURRENTS
Briefing · Week of May 25-30, 2026

A briefing for someone who hasn't been following.

Several long-running stories produced settled-looking conclusions: the crime story has a name, the AI story has a Pope, the Republican economic program has been written down by the Treasury Secretary, the Iran war has institutionally defeated the people who warned against it, and the Democratic Party has a concrete four-piece program in front of it whether or not it picks the program up.

  • The post-2020 crime wave is over, and the pattern has a name. Crime analyst Jeff Asher projects 2026 will set the lowest U.S. homicide rate on record. Baltimore — long the country's worst-case city — went from 348 homicides in 2019 to 133 in 2025; April 2026 produced four, the lowest single month since at least 1970. Four writers from very different orientations (Free Press, Marginal Revolution, Slow Boring, NYT) converged on the same causal stack: focused-deterrence policing (targeting the small predictable group responsible for retaliatory gang shootings), voters replacing "progressive prosecutors" with tougher ones, and a partial reversal of the post-Ferguson police pullback.

  • Pope Leo XIV's debut encyclical on AI, Magnifica Humanitas ("Magnificent Humanity"), prompted a four-publication convergence. Writers in UnHerd, Compact, Persuasion, and the New York Times read it as the Catholic Church stepping onto argumentative ground secular AI skeptics have vacated — warning that grounding human dignity in intelligence makes "speciesist" a coherent insult and the case for AI personhood almost inevitable. Ross Douthat dissented in the NYT, calling the document too cautious about the personhood-and-idolatry challenge that AI-lab researchers themselves are now openly raising.

  • The Republican Party formally repudiated free-trade economics, in writing. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's "While America Slept" speech treated comparative-advantage theory — the foundational case for free trade since the 1820s — as a historical mistake the GOP has now corrected. A separate "shockingly weird Warren-Trump alliance" passed the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, banning large institutional investors from buying existing single-family homes.

  • The Iran war's institutional aftermath is consolidating: the war-skeptics are being defeated even as the war's failures vindicate them. A Free Press profile shows Pentagon strategist Elbridge Colby — hired as the "prioritizer" meant to restrain Middle East entanglement — overrun by his own administration. DNI Tulsi Gabbard resigned; reporter Ken Klippenstein documents that the position was deliberately neutered in 2004, so her exit changes nothing structurally. Stanford political scientist Francis Fukuyama argues drones have now closed the operational middle of warfare entirely — combined-arms maneuver warfare is over until somebody fields cheap anti-drone systems.

  • Matthew Yglesias published a four-piece program for Democrats in a single week: stop ducking the growth question, drop the empty "affordability" rhetoric, re-embrace deficit politics (tax-the-rich plus defend-Social-Security under that frame), and broker an alliance between organized labor and the supply-side "abundance" agenda (housing, nuclear power, infrastructure).

  • The Texas Senate runoff opened a real path for Democrats to flip the U.S. Senate in 2026. Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton's 64-36 demolition of Sen. John Cornyn in the Republican primary — combined with Trump's late, weak endorsement issued at his lowest approval rating — left the GOP nominating a scandal-plagued candidate against Democratic state legislator James Talarico. Forecaster Nate Silver reads it as a self-inflicted Republican wound; Texas hasn't elected a Democratic senator since 1988.

  • Two non-Western windows confirmed the global system is being rewritten. Economic historian Adam Tooze quantified China's 1978-2022 growth surge as 20 times Japan's 1953-1991 takeoff in population lifted out of poverty. Nigerian analyst Nouridin Melo reported the new 650,000-barrel-per-day Dangote Refinery is breaking the colonial-era export-crude / import-refined architecture, exporting refined fuel to five other African states. China Digital Times documented dozens of phrases the Chinese government soft-censored around the May 14-15 Trump-Xi summit Beijing publicly claimed as a victory.

What to watch: whether the Talarico Senate path firms up after Texas's runoff is certified, whether any of the Yglesias program shows up in a 2028 Democratic positioning, and whether the AI-encyclical conversation hardens into the kind of durable taboo Catholic mobilization built around human cloning two decades ago.
