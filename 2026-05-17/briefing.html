The Week: May 10 – 17, 2026

A briefing for someone who hasn’t been following.

A Summit in Beijing the Two Sides Saw Differently

  • President Donald Trump made his first state visit to China since 2017, meeting Xi Jinping in Beijing on May 14–15.
    • Friendly choreography: pledges of roughly 200 Boeing airliners and 400 GE jet engines, talk of a future trade architecture, no audible argument.
    • Substantive ledger looked different: Xi lectured Trump on Taiwan — the self-governing island China claims as its own — and warned of “clashes and even conflicts” if Washington misjudged it.
    • Trump declined to say whether the U.S. would defend Taiwan; on Saturday he warned Taipei against any formal independence declaration.
    • Trump’s pre-summit sanctions on Chinese refiners buying Iranian oil were ignored on Xi’s instructions; Trump then said he was considering lifting them and praised Xi as “very good. He’s not coming in with guns.”
  • The week’s most revealing intelligence was unrelated to the summit itself.
    • Gulf monarchies — the Persian Gulf states that have built their security around American protection for half a century — are now buying Chinese-made air-defense systems for their oil infrastructure.
    • The U.S. exhausted much of its missile-interceptor stockpile failing to remove Iran’s government this spring; the states most exposed to Iranian retaliation drew the obvious lesson.
    • The American war meant to project deterrence improved China’s position in the Persian Gulf.
  • European editorial pages read it the same way.
    • A survey of seven major papers across France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Sweden, Slovenia, and Catalonia found six concluding the relationship is trending in China’s favor.
  • The American debate is split on whether China is rising or peaking.
    • China is peaking now: fertility at one child per woman, four straight years of population decline, 32 percent of Chinese aged 18–24 saying they want no children — Xi can see his window closing, which makes the next few years the most dangerous.
    • Demography measures the wrong thing: China is the world’s dominant producer of ships, EVs, batteries, drones, and machine tools; Chinese AI labs deliver models within months of America’s at a fraction of the price.
    • A separate investigative report this week — half the authors at the top 2022 machine-learning conference earned undergraduate degrees in China; seven of the first eleven hires at Meta’s Superintelligence Labs are Chinese-born — concluded the AI race is one America is winning largely by employing the Chinese diaspora.
  • The bottom line: the U.S. will no longer deter China by demonstration of force. That deterrent got cashed in this spring, and the change came back.

Iran at Day 78

  • The Iran war is now in its eleventh week. The April cease-fire never held; the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly a fifth of the world’s oil passes — remains closed; about 10 percent of global oil supply is off the market.
    • Brent crude is around $107 rather than the $200 most analysts projected, because floating storage, the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve, Chinese inventories, and sanctions waivers bought the market a delay.
    • Rationing has already arrived in Pakistan, Bangladesh, Vietnam, and Sri Lanka.
  • The most consequential development this week was administrative, not military.
    • Iranian lawmakers introduced draft legislation to tax the submarine fiber-optic cables passing through Hormuz — the cables carrying roughly half the internet traffic between the Persian Gulf, Asia, and Europe.
    • Foreign operators would need Iranian authorization and pay transit fees; Iranian companies would monopolize maintenance.
    • An Iranian Army spokesman publicly confirmed it: “We will impose taxes on Internet cables.”
    • Iran’s Revolutionary Guards have divers and underwater drones; pro-government media floated that damage could affect “entire systems” — financial transactions, military communications, AI cloud storage.
    • The Vice President of the Iranian Parliament summed it up: “Hormuz is our nuclear weapon.”
  • Asian navies have stayed almost entirely absent from the U.S.-led effort to reopen the strait — despite Japan sourcing 95 percent of its crude through the Persian Gulf — and the coalition the U.S. would need in a Taiwan conflict cannot form on this run-up.

The Iran War Comes Home to the Press

  • The Justice Department this spring issued grand-jury subpoenas to reporters at the Wall Street Journal, Axios, and the Washington Post over their February reporting on the run-up to the Iran war.
    • The reporting in question documented that General Dan Caine — chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff — had privately warned President Trump, six days before the war began, about the risks the campaign carried.
    • The named risks have since become realities: Iranian attacks on U.S. installations, depletion of American air-defense interceptors, the threat to the Strait of Hormuz.
    • The story angered the president, who complained directly to the deputy attorney general; the subpoenas followed.
    • The Journal disclosed the subpoenas this week.
  • The story landed in the briefing because of who said so, not just what happened.
    • A former federal prosecutor writing in National Review — the institutional-conservative magazine of the American right, founded by William F. Buckley in 1955 — and Ken Klippenstein, an independent investigative journalist who covers national security from the left on Substack, both wrote this week that the administration has crossed an institutional line earlier Justice Departments (including the first Trump one) refrained from crossing.
    • Neither writer usually agrees with the other; the convergence is itself the news.
    • The Justice Department’s longstanding internal policy of higher procedural thresholds for subpoenaing journalists — attorney-general-level approval, exhaustion of other investigative routes, narrow drafting — has been quietly bypassed.
    • The National Review author, who supported the Iran war, framed the erosion as the kind that ultimately makes the case for federal press-shield legislation — the kind he had previously opposed precisely because the Justice Department could be trusted to police itself.
  • Klippenstein’s companion finding is the mirror image, pointed at the right rather than at the mainstream press.
    • The White House’s new counterterrorism czar, Sebastian Gorka, named Tucker Carlson and the streamer Nick Fuentes in a Breitbart interview as potential subjects of the administration’s domestic-terrorism strategy — a strategy whose formal text lists only left-wing groups.
    • Gorka’s mechanism for fitting them in was to declare that Carlson and Fuentes are not really conservatives anyway.
    • Both had recently broken with Trump over the Iran war — Carlson called the strikes “the single biggest mistake Trump, or any American president, has made in my lifetime”; Fuentes posted “ISRAEL IS DRAGGING US INTO WAR. AMERICA FIRST.”
    • The mainstream-press subpoenas and the redefinition of MAGA-aligned dissenters as not-really-conservative-and-therefore-suspect are two operational instruments pointed in opposite directions but enabled by the same predicate — wartime executive prerogative — and triggered by the same offense, which is criticism of the war.
  • Why the convergence matters: press-prosecution stories are easy to dismiss as the press defending itself. When a former federal prosecutor at the magazine of mainstream American conservatism arrives at the same diagnosis as a left-of-center independent national-security reporter, the diagnosis is harder to write off.

Britain Replaces Its Government in Slow Motion

  • Reform UK, the right-populist party led by Nigel Farage, won roughly one in every three contested council seats in early-May local elections across England and Wales.
    • Keir Starmer’s Labour government — elected just last year with a historic 411-seat parliamentary majority — has been polling third behind Reform and the Conservatives.
  • The distinctive feature isn’t the populist rise; it’s the fiscal trap underneath it.
    • In 2022 the Conservative government tried unfunded tax cuts; the bond market revolted; the central bank stood back.
    • Starmer’s team now refuses to try anything that might trigger a repeat. Every economic decision is constrained by what the bond market is presumed to tolerate.
    • The central bank — which actually shapes that tolerance through its own bond-buying decisions — is being treated as an immovable force rather than a policy actor.
    • Italian debt currently trades at lower interest rates than British debt; the difference is largely that the European Central Bank credibly signaled it would intervene and the Bank of England did not.
  • The same demographic and migration pressures that produced Reform UK exist across the European center and the United States — aging populations, mass immigration as the demographic solution, internet-accelerated disillusionment.
    • Britain is the cleanest current example of what they produce when they overwhelm an exhausted incumbent: not a coup or a constitutional crisis, just a successor party nobody took seriously two years ago suddenly being the most plausible governing alternative.

A New Outbreak, a Familiar Reassurance

  • A hantavirus outbreak — the Andes strain, normally rare and rodent-borne — broke out aboard the cruise ship MV Hondius after a South America voyage.
    • Seven cases confirmed; an infected passenger collapsed and died on an April 25 flight to St. Helena.
    • The WHO and U.S. public-health officials issued reassurances: the strain requires “close and prolonged contact” and is unlikely to spread widely.
  • A New York Times science columnist did something simple and damaging — she phoned the senior author of the 2018 Argentinian-outbreak study the WHO was citing.
    • He told her his paper was being misread; “close and prolonged contact” was never meant to rule out airborne transmission.
    • The 2018 outbreak began with one carrier who spent 90 minutes at a 100-person birthday party and infected non-adjacent attendees, then ten more at the wake.
    • Each case infects 2.1 others on average, in the early-2020 coronavirus range; incubation can run 40 days.
    • The WHO quietly updated its technical documents over the weekend.
  • The companion story: Make America Healthy Again (MAHA), the wellness coalition aligned with Health and Human Services Secretary Robert F. Kennedy Jr., destroyed an FDA-approved company on bad information.
    • In April 2023 two Facebook posts confused a grape-derived produce coating from Apeel Sciences — approved by the FDA and its European counterpart — with an unrelated British floor cleaner of the same name.
    • MAHA-aligned influencers piled on, leaning on Bill Gates’s 2012 grant to Apeel as proof of malign intent.
    • Costco, Kroger, Albertsons, Driscoll’s, and the 130-year-old citrus producer Limoneira all dropped Apeel under online pressure.
    • By 2025 the company had no American business left; its European business is thriving because EU law requires social-media platforms to remove demonstrably false content.
  • The shape both stories share: institutions designed to communicate risk have lost the standing to do it, and the movements that filled the gap have no quality control of their own.

The Rules of the Game

  • Last month the Supreme Court ruled in Louisiana v. Callais that race-based congressional districts — including most majority-Black districts created since the 1965 Voting Rights Act — likely violate the Constitution.
    • Combined with a 2019 ruling that put partisan gerrymanders beyond federal review, the decision lets state legislatures redraw districts aggressively as long as they cite partisan rather than racial reasons.
  • The Republican response was fast:
    • Tennessee dismantled the 40-year-old majority-Black 9th District around Memphis.
    • Louisiana’s governor invoked emergency powers to suspend an in-progress primary after 42,000 ballots had been cast.
    • South Carolina is moving against the seat of Representative James Clyburn, one of the country’s most prominent Black politicians.
    • A widely read forecaster who had previously thought Democrats could hold the U.S. House conceded this week that they cannot.
  • The picture is not one-sided.
    • Virginia Democrats passed a constitutional amendment intended to enable one of the most extreme partisan gerrymanders in the country, which the state Supreme Court struck down.
    • Some Virginia Democrats then floated forcing the court into early retirement by lowering its mandatory retirement age.
    • Intent is symmetric; capacity is not — Republicans control more state legislatures positioned to act quickly.
  • The deeper finding, advanced this week by an election-law professor in Compact magazine: American politics has migrated from contesting policy outcomes to contesting the formal terms of acquiring power.

Datacenters Become a Local Politics Story

  • Local opposition to the artificial-intelligence buildout kept growing.
    • The Environmental Protection Agency proposed a rule this week letting datacenter developers begin construction — concrete pads, electrical hookups, support structures — before securing the Clean Air Act permits historically required.
    • Industry view: this shaves months off timelines.
    • Legal objection: it forecloses regulatory leverage at the only stage when leverage exists.
  • The mood is captured by Project Matador, an 11-gigawatt datacenter the Texas company Fermi Americas (backed by former Energy Secretary Rick Perry) is trying to build in Amarillo.
    • Fermi’s CEO was fired by his board last month; aerial photos show no significant construction.
    • A citizens’ group with a legal budget under $2,000 is challenging the permit.
  • Perry Village, Ohio (population 1,600) is the same story in miniature.
    • The mayor and council signed non-disclosure agreements with developers before constituents had any idea a 230-acre datacenter using 200,000 gallons of water a day was being negotiated.
    • When residents gathered enough signatures to force a referendum, the council quietly rescinded and re-enacted the ordinances under emergency provisions designed to defeat it.
    • Ohio is now considering legislation that would bar local governments from passing datacenter-restriction laws at all.
  • Five years from now, the most consequential political effect of the AI boom may turn out to be the local-democracy fight it provoked.

The Bottom Line

  • A story most American readers will not have seen rounds out the week.
    • On April 25 the capital of Mali — a West African country whose northern desert has been contested for fifteen years — suffered a coordinated wave of attacks, including a suicide bombing that killed the country’s defense minister.
    • The attackers were a previously-unimaginable alliance between a Tuareg separatist movement and a regional al-Qaeda affiliate.
    • African and Arabic-language analysts read the convergence as evidence that an underlying political question — Tuareg self-rule, suppressed but never settled by France’s 2013 intervention, a 2015 peace accord, two military coups, and Russian mercenary contracts — has found the militant channel available to it.
  • That pattern is the thread of the week.
    • The Beijing summit, the Hormuz cable-tax bill, the Sahel reorganization, and the Asian navies’ absence from the Persian Gulf are all examples of the same architectural shift.
    • Institutions, supply chains, and security arrangements that depended on American leadership are being replaced by ones that do not.
    • None of the replacements require American consent; most are operational already.
  • The fights loudest at home — WHO/MAHA, redistricting, datacenter permits — are variations on a single problem.
    • Institutions designed to mediate disagreement have lost the standing to do it.
    • The coalitions filling the void have no quality control of their own.
    • The trajectory in all three is toward formal-rules contests as a substitute for the substantive ones the institutions can no longer host.
    • Britain is the cleanest preview of what that looks like a decade in.
  • American institutional capacity to recover is the largest unmeasured variable in the period. This week’s evidence is not that it has started recovering.
