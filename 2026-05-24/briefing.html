The Week: May 18 – 24, 2026 (Bullet Form)

A briefing for someone who hasn’t been following.

The “Anti-Weaponization Fund”

  • President Donald Trump’s January lawsuit against the Internal Revenue Service (over leaks of his 2018-2020 tax returns) was settled on May 19, with $1.776 billion routed from the federal Judgment Fund into a new “Anti-Weaponization Fund.”
    • Trump was the plaintiff; his administration controlled the defendant. Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche — Trump’s former personal lawyer — approved the settlement.
    • The fund is administered by five officials Trump can fire at will. Recipients and amounts may be kept secret.
    • Blanche added a clause permanently barring the U.S. from bringing any future civil or criminal action against Trump, his sons, or the Trump Organization on any pending or potential federal matter.
    • The Judgment Fund is a 1956 permanent appropriation Congress created for court-ordered judgments; over the decades it was quietly amended to cover negotiated settlements with no dollar cap.
    • The presiding judge had flagged the “a man cannot sue himself” problem and signaled a hearing. The settlement was announced first; the judge then closed the case noting the settlement was not on the record.
  • Legal commentators across the political range — a former federal prosecutor at National Review, a younger conservative at the same magazine, and a Yale constitutional-law scholar at the centrist Free Press — converged on the same diagnostic.
    • The settlement is probably technically legal. Nobody obvious has standing to sue. The wrongs are political and structural, not criminal.
    • The Supreme Court’s 2024 ruling on presidential immunity (Trump v. United States) makes most such abuses unprosecutable even when criminal.
    • The remedy available is congressional: impeachment, oversight, repealing the Judgment Fund’s structure. Republicans control Congress and will do none of these. The constitutional system has a vulnerability it was not designed to handle.
  • Parallel developments the same week:
    • The Securities and Exchange Commission rescinded a 54-year-old gag rule that had let it publicly trumpet charges it had not proven in court while preventing settling defendants from defending themselves outside court.
    • Independent reporter Ken Klippenstein documented that the administration’s claim of having killed 860 “jihadis” in 15 months is unverifiable by design. The flagship case — a Trump-announced strike on “the second in command of ISIS globally” in Nigeria — involved a Nigerian Boko Haram veteran whose global ranking was largely a U.S. press talking point, in an operation Nigerian forces actually conducted.

The Democratic Autopsy

  • The Democratic National Committee released its long-delayed autopsy of the 2024 presidential election on Thursday, after CNN obtained a leaked copy.
    • Commissioned by new DNC chair Ken Martin from a Democratic consultant working part-time as a volunteer. Sat on for months while donors and progressives speculated about what it contained.
  • What was in it turned out to be more embarrassing than the speculation:
    • Zero references to “Israel” or “Gaza” anywhere in 193 pages.
    • Opening disclaimer that the DNC could not verify its own assertions; hostile internal annotations contesting claims throughout; no conclusion section.
    • Substantive findings were thin: Harris lost Black male voters because she didn’t address economic concerns; working-class voters support Democratic economic policies but won’t vote for Democratic candidates; Trump’s attack ads on transgender policy were “very effective” and nothing could have been done.
    • One case-study attributed the North Carolina Democratic gubernatorial candidate’s outperformance to “Harris being a bad candidate” rather than to his opponent being a former lieutenant governor with a documented history of pornographic-message-board posts.
  • Four writers across the political range read the document.
    • A New York Times columnist concluded the autopsy’s incompetence is itself the story — Martin turned a screw-up into a crisis, the DNC is approaching insolvency under his leadership, and replacements are being discussed.
    • A Free Press writer (former Democratic fundraiser who voted Trump in 2024) argued the autopsy could not be honest about race-and-class findings because the progressive base will not permit the conversation.
    • A National Review essayist noted party autopsies are historically useless anyway, but treated this one as a leading indicator of organizational decay.
    • A separate National Review piece by Noah Rothman documented that a February Axios story had cited advocacy groups claiming the DNC was suppressing Gaza-cost-Harris findings — and that, when the actual report contained no such finding, the activist response was to convert the disconfirmation into further confirmation.
  • Rothman’s empirical case against the Gaza-cost-Harris claim is rigorous: down-ballot Democrats with identical Israel positions outperformed Harris in the same states; the Michigan “uncommitted” Muslim vote was too small statistically; the loss was overdetermined by inflation, immigration, Biden’s age, and Harris’s own political-talent deficit.
    • But Rothman overshoots when he pivots from “Gaza was not the marginal cause” to a sweeping claim that the activist left is rising through “intimidation, menace, and the selective application of force.” That is a much larger argument than the column does the work to support.

The Beijing Summit, A Week Later

  • Trump’s May 15 meeting with Chinese President Xi Jinping produced specific commercial deliverables — two limited market-opening agreements, an unconfirmed $17 billion Chinese agricultural pledge, a 200-aircraft Boeing order — and an effort to claim as a “win” that China had agreed the Strait of Hormuz (the narrow waterway through which about a fifth of the world’s oil passes) should be demilitarized and un-tolled long-term. As one commentator noted, no prior American president would have considered making that ask.
  • The optics were unmistakable.
    • Trump publicly praised Xi as “great leader,” “really a friend,” “from central casting” — phrasings Xi did not reciprocate.
    • Trump suggested Taiwan, the self-governing island China claims, was negotiable: “we’re supposed to travel 9,500 miles to fight a war.”
  • Stanford political theorist Francis Fukuyama argued American decline since 2016 is largely a product of Trump’s own actions — polarization, science funding cuts, the Iran war that drained advanced-weapons stocks, and an administration whose officials insist domestic Democrats are a bigger threat than China.
    • His counterintuitive prediction: Xi may not move on Taiwan while Trump is in office, precisely because Trump’s self-inflicted damage is doing Xi’s work. The window of danger is the next presidency — when a successor trying to reverse course might force Xi to act before the reversal succeeds.
  • Europe was not at the table. A foreign-policy realist writing in Hungarian Conservative and a center-left writer at Slow Boring (genuinely opposite political coordinates) converged on the same diagnostic.
    • Trump’s actual China policy is incoherent protectionism: surrenders chip-export leverage, alienates allies any serious China strategy would require, sabotages domestic energy buildout (Chinese-derivative nuclear reactors now being built in China on a 56-month timeline; comparable American projects took 15 years), and treats trade deficits as the relevant metric.
    • The world being assembled around the summit’s empty European chair is structural reordering — European interests increasingly set by Washington and Beijing without European input.
  • Two non-English-language windows confirmed the read.
    • A Moroccan political scientist writing in Arabic for Al Jazeera: the post-1945 architecture lacks the Cold War’s stabilizing infrastructure — no equivalent of the 1963 Moscow-Washington hotline, no codified rules. “The balance of terror has been replaced by the terror of imbalance.”
    • A retired Tsinghua University sociologist writing in Chinese on a banned WeChat account walks Chinese readers through American historian Robert Kagan’s argument that an “anti-liberal hidden thread” has run through 250 years of American politics. Chinese liberal-academic discourse is now reading Trump as the surfacing of a structural feature, not as an electoral anomaly.
    • China Digital Times translated comments on a now-banned Chinese video that mocked Xi by allegory (“a tortoise on a utility pole couldn’t have climbed there itself”). A striking sub-thread invoked foreign strikes by name — “B2,” “F35,” “bald eagle” — citing Iran and Venezuela as precedents. Small slice; should not be over-read; but the first time this specific form of anti-Xi sentiment has been visible in Chinese-language internet comments.

Iran at Three Months

  • The Iran war, in its third month after February airstrikes that killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, continues to grind. Trump announced Saturday that an agreement was “largely negotiated”; by Sunday he told negotiators “not to rush.” Reports of imminent agreements have been frequent enough across two months that markets and analysts treat them cautiously.
    • The deal under discussion: 60-day extension of the existing ceasefire, reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, continued negotiations over Iran’s nuclear program.
  • Two pieces of analysis outside the daily news cycle stood out.
    • Oxford historian Peter Frankopan traced 200 years of foreign interventions in Iran — Russian and British concessions, the 1953 CIA-backed coup against Prime Minister Mohammad Mosaddegh, the installation of the Pahlavi monarchy — to argue the Islamic Republic’s posture (asymmetric warfare, sanctions-resistant trade with China and Russia, strategic ambiguity on nuclear enrichment) is shaped less by clerical doctrine than by the regime’s reading of two centuries of evidence about what happens when Iran is weak.
    • University of Tennessee political scientist Saeid Golkar dismantled a New York Times report that the U.S. and Israel had considered installing former Iranian president Mahmoud Ahmadinejad as Khamenei’s replacement. Iran is not a leader-centered regime; it is a “theocratic-security state” in which the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) — controlling between a fifth and a third of the economy — answers only to the Supreme Leader. Even when Ahmadinejad was president, the IRGC sided with Khamenei when the two clashed and placed Ahmadinejad under house arrest.
  • The structural worry: if Washington misread Iran’s regime at the start of the war, it is likely to misread what kind of exit is achievable at the end.

Britain and the Far Right

  • The far-right activist Tommy Robinson (born Stephen Yaxley, co-founder of the now-defunct English Defence League) organized a “Unite the Kingdom” march in central London on May 16. An estimated 60,000 attended.
    • Comes two weeks after Nigel Farage’s Reform UK won roughly a third of contested council seats in local elections; both Labour and the Conservatives posted abysmal numbers.
  • A reporter for Persuasion spent the day on the ground and returned with a more textured account than the wire-service coverage.
    • The coalition was heterogeneous: an Iranian couple opposed to Sharia law, an Indian student worried about Islam in Britain, a German-Hindu business owner with Afghan parents, working-class Brexit voters from the Midlands, Christian evangelicals with crucifixes, British Trump admirers, Iranian monarchists with images of the late Shah’s son.
    • Convergent grievance: radical Islam as civilizational threat, anchored to specific recent events — the multi-region “grooming gangs” scandals (police slow to investigate organized abuse of young girls by men of South Asian Muslim background, for fear of appearing racist), the asylum-hotel system that expanded after Brexit, and the 2024 Southport stabbings of three young girls (which triggered a week of riots after Robinson amplified the false rumor the killer was a Muslim asylum seeker).
  • The reporter granted the underlying concerns are not pure invention but argued the crowd inflated them into “a vast narrative of terminal decline and elite betrayal.”
    • Structural reading: this is not a fading street phenomenon. The coalition is broader, more religious, and more electorally serious than the previous decade’s Robinson activism suggested. Members will vote Reform UK only because no party further right is viable.
    • Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer is widely expected to be replaced within the year — not by another Labour figure, but by Farage.
  • Related European stories.
    • Russian (and disputed Ukrainian) large drones have repeatedly crossed into Estonian, Latvian, and Lithuanian airspace. NATO shot one down over an Estonian lake — the first such action. Vilnius issued an official air-raid alert and discovered shelter capacity had run out.
    • A European-press digest returned a striking cross-orientation consensus that the frontline Baltic states have no civil-defense plan, no legal authorization to shoot down drones, and no functional means to do so.
    • German Chancellor Friedrich Merz proposed an “associate EU membership” for Ukraine — attendance at EU decision-making bodies without voting rights — drawing mixed reactions: symbolic anchor, face-saving fiction for what is legally impossible during wartime, or, in the Ukrainian press, a “waiting room” imposing EU obligations without conferring EU rights.

Massie, the Donor Class, and the Republican Coalition

  • Rep. Thomas Massie (R-Ky.) lost his Republican primary on Tuesday to Ed Gallrein, a former Navy SEAL personally recruited by Trump.
    • The race attracted more than $34 million in outside spending, much of it from pro-Israel groups, making it the most expensive House primary in U.S. history.
    • Massie was a libertarian-leaning Republican who had opposed the Iran war, voted against the administration’s signature tax package, criticized Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu, and (per Trump’s reported view, the breaking point) co-led with Democratic Rep. Ro Khanna the bipartisan push to force release of the federal Epstein investigation files.
  • Three writers across three orientations read the outcome differently.
    • A Heritage Foundation conservative-thought fellow at the post-liberal Compact treated the defeat as the GOP imposing overdue discipline on a strain of opposition Republicanism obsolete now that the party has both a coherent program and a working majority.
    • A center-left columnist at UnHerd read it as confirming Trump’s total personalist dominance fused with pro-Israel hawkishness as the operative ideological filter — with AIPAC and Republican Jewish groups defending the GOP as their “last stand” because Democrats under 50 are turning against Israel.
    • Klippenstein focused on the candidate: Gallrein refused debates, citing his “classified” SEAL background as the entire warrant for his fitness for office. Klippenstein paired him with the Democratic “national-security moms” (former CIA officers and military veterans in Congress) to argue the national-security state is increasingly its own source of political legitimacy, floating above democratic accountability.
  • The three readings agreed the same forces would have produced the same outcome against any equally-positioned dissenter. They disagreed on whose dysfunction it was — Massie’s, the donors’, or the political form itself.
  • Three days after losing, Massie told NBC the Republican Party would be “very vulnerable” in the 2026 midterms and that he had not ruled out a 2028 presidential run.

AI Becomes a Local Politics Story

  • Several AI threads converged this week into what looks like a tipping point.
  • Polling.
    • Only 18% of Generation Z reports feeling hopeful about AI; 47% of U.S. voters under 30 rate it “mostly bad,” the highest of any age bracket.
    • A Stanford survey of 30 countries found Americans had the lowest trust of any surveyed population in their leaders’ ability to regulate the technology.
    • GOP pollster Chris Wilson told Heatmap News that Republican opposition to AI data centers has risen more than 300% in some GOP regions over six months — reaching levels offshore-wind opposition once did.
    • A serious 2028 Republican presidential candidate, the analysis predicted, will run on a national data-center moratorium.
  • Mechanics.
    • The Environmental Protection Agency proposed a rule letting data-center developers begin construction before securing the air-pollution permits historically required.
    • Republican elected officials in red states (a South Carolina representative, the Texas agriculture commissioner, a North Dakota Public Service Commission candidate) have proposed moratoria.
    • Right-wing online figures — Tucker Carlson, Marjorie Taylor Greene, Matt Walsh — have started attacking data-center water and power demands.
    • The administration’s counter-frame, embraced by Interior Secretary Doug Burgum, is that the backlash is foreign-funded — the same framing Republicans tried unsuccessfully against rural solar opposition.
  • Ideology.
    • A libertarian economist at the Free Press argued the political energy that ran the 2018-2022 progressive cultural moment has migrated onto material targets, and AI infrastructure is the principal one.
    • The Vatican’s International Theological Commission published its first document mentioning artificial general intelligence. Pope Leo XIV announced he would address AI governance in an upcoming encyclical (a major papal teaching document), with a former Anthropic co-founder advising him.
    • A Compact essay traced the analogy to human cloning: at the turn of the millennium, Vatican documents and domestic laws built a durable taboo that has restrained governments for two decades. A similar mobilization could be attempted against superintelligence.
  • Bipartisan policy actually moving.
    • The Senate Judiciary Committee unanimously advanced the GUARD Act, co-sponsored by Republican Sen. Josh Hawley and Democratic Sen. Richard Blumenthal, requiring age verification for AI companion chatbots.
    • Bill emerged in response to several high-profile lawsuits over teen suicides allegedly encouraged by chatbots. Closes a loophole inherited from a 1998 federal privacy law that effectively required companies to have seen a child’s birth certificate before legal liability attached.
  • Local mechanics in one Ohio town.
    • In Perry Village (population 1,600), the mayor and council had signed nondisclosure agreements with data-center developers before constituents had any idea a 230-acre facility using 200,000 gallons of water a day was being negotiated.
    • When residents gathered enough signatures to force a referendum, the council quietly rescinded and re-enacted the rezoning under emergency provisions designed to defeat the petition.
    • Ohio’s state legislature is now considering a bill that would bar local governments from passing data-center restrictions at all.
    • Five years from now, the most consequential political effect of the AI boom may be the local-democracy fight it provoked.

Smaller But Important

  • Cuba: the administration’s pressure campaign on Havana — naval blockade, sanctions on the Cuban military’s commercial conglomerate, a secret May 14 visit by CIA director John Ratcliffe — combined with the Justice Department’s superseding criminal indictment of 94-year-old Raúl Castro for the 1996 Cuban shootdown of two unarmed civilian Cessna aircraft operated by the Miami-based group Brothers to the Rescue.
    • Shoot-down killed four men over international waters; cockpit recordings captured the Cuban pilots celebrating with “¡Patria o Muerte!”
    • Castro will almost certainly never enter a Miami courtroom. The Free Press writer argued the indictment has independent value as a public, durable legal account that corrects a Netflix dramatization that recast the Cuban spy ring as patriotic underdogs.
  • Texas Senate runoff scheduled May 26 now widely seen as in play for the Democratic nominee, James Talarico, a state legislator and Presbyterian seminarian.
    • Forecaster Nate Silver’s analysts argued the chain of events was self-inflicted: Trump’s initial co-endorsement of all three Republican primary candidates defeated the informational purpose of an endorsement; he waited until the final week to single-endorse Attorney General Ken Paxton, after both campaigns had burned more than $20 million in primary ad spending; Paxton (scandal-plagued, in the model of Georgia’s failed 2022 Senate candidate Herschel Walker) is the weaker general-election option.
    • Texas has not elected a Democratic senator since 1988.
  • Ezra Klein interviewed political scientist Lee Drutman on the gerrymandering arms race underway across roughly a dozen states after recent Supreme Court rulings cleared both partisan gerrymanders and most race-based districts.
    • Drutman’s argument: the underlying cause is the structure of single-member winner-take-all districts. The fix is open-list proportional representation in multi-member districts — a 40% Republican vote share in a state yields roughly 40% of that state’s House seats, gerrymandering becomes mechanically inert, and new parties become viable at lower thresholds.
    • Politically far from feasible. But the only structural alternative now on the table to indefinite escalation.

The Bottom Line

  • The threads share a shape. The “Anti-Weaponization Fund,” the DNC autopsy’s incompetence, the Beijing summit’s quiet European exclusion, the Massie defeat, the AI infrastructure backlash spreading from local zoning fights to presidential-primary positioning — all are stories about institutional capacity decaying faster than the public debate has registered.
  • The constitutional system has discovered it cannot easily defend itself against a settlement of the federal government’s lawsuit against itself.
  • The opposition party has discovered it cannot conduct a basic post-election review without exposing how thinly it is staffed.
  • The post-1945 multilateral architecture has discovered it can be replaced by bilateral deals nobody in Europe is at the table for.
  • The Republican coalition has discovered the donor class can still buy a primary but no longer commands the rising generation of its own voters.
  • The AI industry has discovered its political license to operate is being revoked one rural Ohio county at a time.
  • None of these are settled stories. What is striking, week to week, is how often credible analysts from genuinely different political starting points reach the same diagnostic before disagreeing about what to do about it.
  • That convergence is, on its own, a reading of the moment: the surface-level partisan fights are increasingly the noise, and the structural drift underneath them is increasingly the signal.
