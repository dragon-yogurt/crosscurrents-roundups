A briefing for someone who hasn’t been following.

A Caltech Engineer Tries to Kill the President

  • Late on Saturday, April 25, Cole Tomas Allen, a 31-year-old California aerospace engineer, attempted to enter the Washington Hilton armed, reportedly intending to kill President Trump and members of his Cabinet at the White House Correspondents’ Dinner — the annual black-tie gathering of the press corps and the political class. He was stopped before reaching the ballroom; no one was injured.

  • Allen does not fit any movement profile.

    • Caltech graduate (mechanical engineering, 2017), worked at a precision-engineering firm building stabilization hardware for drones, self-described devout Christian.
    • Never flagged by the FBI’s domestic counterterrorism apparatus — the surveillance state’s posture is tuned to catch exactly this kind of attacker, and it didn’t.
    • His own manifesto cites the Sermon on the Mount and frames the killing as personal moral self-purification — closer to the December 2024 shooter who killed UnitedHealthcare CEO Brian Thompson than to any ideologically organized assassination.
    • Reporting on his social-media history showed his contempt for the political system was bipartisan, including repeated calls to replace the Democratic Party.

  • The interpretive landscape has roughly four poles, none of which explain the others:

    • A “normie liberal terrorist” reading: Allen as evidence that ordinary opposition rhetoric is producing the gun in his hand.
    • A 70-year-pattern reading: Allen as the latest in a long chain of leftist political violence; demands the federal government respond at scale, as the Biden Justice Department did to January 6, as a deterrent.
    • A refusal-to-trade reading: the fact that Trump was the target cannot become a reason to euphemize his depravity, only to reiterate that depravity does not justify violence.
    • A “postmodern symptom” reading: Allen and the December UnitedHealthcare shooter together as evidence of educated young professionals, squeezed by a tech-aristocracy class system that has no place for them, killing not for any collective political project but as private moral acts.

  • The diagnostic is the muted public reaction. Within hours, social media had returned to the NBA playoffs. Attempted presidential assassinations no longer stop the world.

  • Two days earlier, 78-year-old Maine Governor Janet Mills withdrew from the Democratic primary for U.S. Senate after losing 27 points to Graham Platner, a 41-year-old oyster farmer with no prior elected experience. Both events sit in the same well — deep public contempt for geriatric establishment institutions, expressed through electoral mechanics where it can be expressed and through more disturbing channels where it can’t.

The Iran War Enters Its Financial Phase, and the Strait Stays Closed

  • The Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which roughly 20 percent of the world’s oil moves — remains effectively closed by Iranian capacity to attack tankers at will, more than a month after the official cease-fire.

    • Brent crude is around $126 a barrel.
    • U.S. gasoline is at $4.18 a gallon.
    • The U.S. has 21 warships in the region — the largest deployment since the 2003 Iraq War.

  • The legal framing of the cease-fire does not survive contact with the facts.

    • The Defense Secretary told the Senate the cease-fire has paused the War Powers Act’s 60-day clock on undeclared U.S. military action.
    • There is no actual cease-fire — Iran is still attacking ships, the U.S. is itself blockading Iran, and a blockade has been recognized as an act of war for centuries.
    • President Trump separately declared the War Powers Act “totally unconstitutional,” which is functionally an admission that the cease-fire claim doesn’t hold.

  • Two analytic camps sharpened this week:

    • Time favors the U.S.: Iran’s economy is breaking, the U.S. holds the technological edge, and pressure will eventually force concessions.
    • Time favors Iran: U.S. munitions stockpiles are also depleting, the Iranian regime has consolidated rather than fractured, and the strait simply cannot be reopened militarily.
    • Both camps largely agree on the underlying picture. They disagree only on which side first runs out of room.

  • A piece from a longtime European-finance observer treats a recent U.S. Treasury emergency dollar swap line to the United Arab Emirates as a tell that Western institutions themselves are under stress they aren’t acknowledging — the UAE doesn’t need the dollars, so the request signals something closer to a 2008-scale early warning than routine cooperation.

  • A useful reframing: the “Iran-as-puppet-master” model is wrong.

    • American discourse defaults to a binary contest between U.S. military power and Iranian “proxy” leverage, with Hezbollah in Lebanon understood as Iran’s puppet.
    • The reality is closer to mutual dependence: Hezbollah’s military weakening in late 2024 is what made direct Israeli planning against Iran conceivable in the first place.
    • Iran cannot be seen abandoning its longest-standing partner without unraveling the entire regional alliance system.
    • Iran’s insistence on Lebanon’s inclusion in any deal is not bluster — it’s the binding constraint. No American framework currently includes this possibility.

The Politics of Electrons

  • The clearest cross-publication finding of the week was that AI data centers — the physical substrate required to train and run advanced AI models — have become a bipartisan local-political liability.

    • A major Virginia data-center project backed by the asset manager Brookfield was canceled after Prince William County resistance to tax breaks.
    • In Trenton, Ohio (in Vice President Vance’s home county, which Trump won 63 percent), roughly 98 percent of polled residents oppose a proposed 250-megawatt facility.
    • A statewide Ohio ballot initiative would ban data centers larger than 25 megawatts.
    • Reports of one congressional aide’s home being shot at and an OpenAI office burned by an arsonist suggest the discontent has extreme fringe edges.

  • The largest U.S. electricity market reopened its interconnection queue this week after a four-year freeze and produced a different mix:

    • By capacity, natural gas alone accounted for nearly half — a sharp shift from the renewable-dominated backlog.
    • The reason is not policy hostility to solar. Data-center load growth has driven average new-project size from 60 megawatts in 2022 to 272 megawatts today, and at gigawatt scale, renewables physically cannot deliver the firm power on the timeline required.

  • A separate report noted the world’s solar growth rate accelerated to 30 percent last year — globally, clean power covered 100 percent of new electricity demand. The two stories sit beside each other because U.S. AI demand has scrambled the global trend.

  • For non-followers, the takeaway: the AI economy’s most politically consequential impact at the moment is not job displacement but electricity prices and grid strain. Five years from now this is likely the issue voters will remember.

The Court Strikes Down Race-Based Districting

  • The Supreme Court ruled 6-3 in Louisiana v. Callais that the state’s deliberately drawn second majority-Black congressional district violated the Constitution’s guarantee of equal protection. The opinion goes further than the Louisiana facts and suggests that most majority-minority districts created since the 1965 Voting Rights Act may now be vulnerable.

  • The legal argument is genuinely contested.

    • Justice Alito’s opinion holds that classifying voters by race for districting purposes is constitutionally suspect even when done to remedy under-representation.
    • Justice Kagan’s dissent makes the corollary point: in the 2019 Rucho decision the Court held that partisan gerrymanders are constitutional and beyond federal-court review. In combination with the new ruling, this lets a state legislature break up a Black urban population into six pieces and dilute its voting power to zero, as long as the legislature claims it was doing so for partisan rather than racial reasons.
    • Even commentary supportive of the new ruling concedes Kagan is correct on this technical point. The deeper dispute is whether race-conscious districting is itself a long-term cure or part of the disease.

  • The same week, a coordinated mid-decade redistricting fight continued across multiple states:

    • Last month Virginia voters approved a Democratic-drawn map that political analysts on both sides called one of the most extreme partisan gerrymanders in the country, in response to Republicans having broken the norm against mid-decade redistricting in Texas, North Carolina, Missouri, and Ohio over the past year.
    • In Utah, the Republican legislature has expanded the state Supreme Court from five to seven justices and is contesting the retention of two sitting justices after a 2024 ruling against legislative gerrymandering.
    • In Maine, Senate Minority Leader Schumer pushed Mills into the Senate race specifically to block Platner; that effort just collapsed.

  • The constitutional architecture now favors partisan-only redistricting at exactly the moment both parties have abandoned the norm against it.

The Democratic Party Searches for Itself

  • The setup: Trump’s job approval is in the high 30s; Democrats now lead Republicans on economic trust for the first time since 2010. The party that may inherit the next government has done very little to earn it.

  • The Mills-Platner result is the lens. Two readings are operating at full volume in their respective media ecosystems:

    • Righteous voter fury at a feckless Democratic establishment that recruited a 78-year-old governor for a Senate seat she would have entered at 79 and finished a first term at 86.
    • A coverup of recently surfaced disqualifying material — a Marines-era drunken tattoo resembling a Nazi symbol, old social-media posts including the slogan “all cops are bastards” — followed by Democratic ranks closing around Platner anyway.

  • Beyond the candidate, the generational shift is real. The median age of Democratic candidates in competitive Senate races has dropped from 63 in the 2018 midterms to 45.5 today.

  • The deeper diagnosis from a second cluster of pieces: Democrats are positioned to win in 2026 on Trump-backlash without having actually moderated the policy positions that lost them 2024 — on immigration, crime, energy, and gender.

    • The diagnostic is Kamala Harris’s persistent polling lead in early 2028 Democratic primary surveys: the party cannot honestly explain why she lost the first time and will struggle to deny her the second.
    • Image-based course corrections — bearded oyster farmer, Trump-style insurgents like California Governor Gavin Newsom — are running ahead of any actual policy substitution.

  • The orthodox technocratic agenda nobody will pursue (cataloged this week):

    • Rebuild IRS enforcement gutted by the Trump administration.
    • End the gimmicky tax exclusions that distort revenue.
    • Crack down on Medicare Advantage upcoding scams.
    • Repeal a 1920 protectionist shipping law called the Jones Act.
    • Both party coalitions have organized around projects voters did not actually ask for.

  • The populist alternative is now in legislative form. Progressive Senate Democrats this week proposed:

    • Automatically reversing every Trump-era corporate merger above $10 billion (Paramount-Warner Bros., HP-Juniper, Google-Wiz, Union Pacific-Norfolk Southern, others).
    • Banning personalized “surveillance pricing” (algorithms that charge different consumers different prices for the same item based on personal data).
    • Federal manufacture of generic drugs.
    • This is the agenda that may drive the 2028 primary if the bipartisan establishment doesn’t first dilute it.

AI: The Bubble Argument Loses, and the Layoffs Begin

  • A widely respected magazine writer who declared an AI bubble seven months ago publicly retracted this week. The reason is straightforward:

    • The agentic coding tools released last fall — AI systems that take over a developer’s machine and complete in minutes work that previously took weeks — have produced concrete evidence of a sustained productivity jump.
    • One leading AI company’s annual revenue run rate grew from $14 billion to $30 billion in two months.
    • A model so powerful it has not been publicly released found decades-old cybersecurity vulnerabilities in production software.
    • The skeptics’ bear case has narrowed to: “this works in coding, where the data is unusually rich and verification is unusually clean — it may not generalize.”

  • The first signs of generalization arrived as 52,000 white-collar tech layoffs in the first quarter of 2026, up 40 percent year over year.

    • Software employment has fallen back to 2017 levels.
    • The class hit hardest is the credentialed symbolic-analyst workforce that ran the post-1980 American economy — finance, software, marketing, consulting.
    • The class doing relatively well is the skilled trades — structural shortages, rising wages.

  • The political consequence: professional-class blue-state workers, suddenly exposed, are radicalizing leftward; trade-school enrollment is up 10 percent since 2020; the geography of opportunity is shifting toward Texas, Oklahoma, the Sun Belt, and the lower Midwest.

  • The dispute over what AI actually is has become more pointed:

    • Modest concrete benefits: a randomized controlled trial of a cheap AI mental-health app among nearly 2,000 Mexican women found a 0.3-standard-deviation improvement in mental health, sustained even after most users stopped engaging.
    • Naked self-interest: the CEO of the major chip manufacturer Nvidia has been arguing that the U.S. should sell its top AI chips to China; one widely read economic commentator scored Nvidia’s case as transparently profiteering.
    • An openly stated will to power: the CEO of the data-analytics firm Palantir released a corporate manifesto announcing that “the nuclear age is over and an era of AI deterrence has begun” — language European newspapers have called the dawn of technofascism.
    • One Turkish editorialist responded that every country must now run its own AI-deterrence calculus rather than accept the American framing.

Hungary, the Sahel, and Europe Repositions

  • Three months after Viktor Orbán’s 16-year rule of Hungary ended in a parliamentary election won by insurgent Péter Magyar, the country’s transition is becoming legible.
    • The new government has a two-thirds parliamentary supermajority.
    • It is expected to reverse Orbán’s anti-Ukrainian, pro-Russian, anti-EU positioning on all three fronts.
    • But Magyar inherits a state apparatus packed for long terms — judges, prosecutors, media regulators, university trustees — that will resist reform structurally.
    • Some commentators in central European papers report members of Orbán’s circle are quietly leaving the country with their wealth, suggesting what looked like a system is showing signs of collapse rather than transition.
  • The aftermath is restructuring European far-right politics:
    • Marine Le Pen reportedly told her party: “We must keep our distance” from the Trump administration.
    • The far-right German party Alternative für Deutschland conceded the same point.
    • Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni’s government has shifted toward the European “strategic autonomy” position long associated with French Gaullists — the project of an EU that can act militarily and economically without depending on Washington.
    • The unifying language is no longer ideological alignment with American conservatism but pragmatic calculation about what U.S. foreign policy actually delivers.
  • In Africa, this week marked the first major roster engagement with the April 25 coordinated attacks across Mali by an al-Qaeda-affiliated jihadist group, in alliance with Tuareg separatist forces.
    • The attacks struck five major cities, killed Mali’s defense minister, and seized the northern town of Kidal.
    • The Sahel — the band of African countries south of the Sahara — now accounts for roughly half of all global terrorism deaths.
    • Western counter-terrorism withdrew over the past three years (France from Mali in 2022, the U.S. from Niger in 2024) and was replaced by Russian mercenaries, who have performed worse on the metrics they were hired for.
    • The jihadist group now governs territory, levies taxes, and generates roughly $30 million annually through extortion and mining taxation.
    • The structural argument: state-controlled jihadist sanctuary qualitatively transforms operational capability — the same asset that gave al-Qaeda the years it needed to plan 9/11 from Taliban Afghanistan.

The Bottom Line

  • Three threads bind the week.

  • First, U.S. institutional power continues to be remade through executive workarounds:

    • The swap-line architecture with the United Arab Emirates.
    • The assertion that the War Powers Act has been paused by a cease-fire that doesn’t exist.
    • The federal biometric build-out.
    • At exactly the moment a major Supreme Court ruling shifts redistricting power from federal courts to state legislatures.

  • Second, the AI economy is no longer hypothetical:

    • Tens of thousands of white-collar layoffs.
    • Electricity-price politics now organizing local elections.
    • The first concrete evidence that productivity gains are real.

  • Third, the political order that organized the post-1945 world is reorganizing in real time:

    • Hungary’s right-populist model has lost an election.
    • European far-right parties are publicly disowning the Trump administration.
    • The United States is voluntarily vacating positions of structural advantage faster than any rival could displace it.

  • What to watch in the coming weeks:

    • Whether a Federal Reserve under new leadership begins dismantling the post-2008 institutional inheritance.
    • Whether the Supreme Court ruling on race-based districting produces the predicted wave of state-level remap suits before the 2026 midterms.
    • Whether the canceled Compass-Brookfield project in Virginia is followed by other major data-center exits.
    • Whether the post-Orbán Hungarian government’s first months in office validate the lesson European centrists are drawing from his defeat.
